# Computer Society of India (CSI), VIT

*sep 2023 — present*

I joined CSI during my first year at VIT, initially because I just wanted to be around people who were genuinely building things.

A lot of college clubs end up becoming mostly about certificates, LinkedIn posts, or surface-level events. What made CSI different for me was that there were actually people trying to create a real technical culture around development, competitive programming, hackathons, systems, AI, open source, and engineering in general.

Over time I got more involved with organizing and running events, eventually moving into leadership roles and later becoming Chairperson.

One thing I genuinely liked about working in CSI was that it wasn't limited to one domain. On any given week we could be:
- running competitive programming contests
- organizing hackathons
- hosting AI or cloud workshops
- collaborating with communities and companies
- helping juniors get started with development
- handling event infrastructure and coordination
- bringing industry speakers and developer communities into campus

A lot of the work ended up being much more operational than people usually expect:
- coordinating teams
- handling sponsorships
- planning technical sessions
- managing volunteers
- communicating with external communities
- organizing logistics for large events
- balancing technical quality with execution constraints

That side of engineering communities honestly taught me a lot about leadership and execution under pressure.

---

## Building technical communities

One of the bigger goals for us was trying to make the technical ecosystem inside college feel more connected and active instead of fragmented into isolated groups.

We collaborated with multiple developer communities and organizations across different domains including:
- CNCF community meetups
- Grafana-focused sessions and observability talks
- cybersecurity-focused(CYBERFRAT) events
- hackathons and problem-solving contests
- design and product-oriented workshops
- cloud-native and systems-oriented sessions

We also worked alongside various external communities, student organizations, startups, and developer groups throughout different events and initiatives.

A lot of these events weren't just lectures — the focus was usually on getting people to actually build, participate, compete, or explore things outside coursework.

---

## Competitive programming and hackathons

Competitive programming was always a strong part of the culture around CSI.

We organized:
- coding contests
- DSA sessions
- beginner-friendly CP workshops
- interview preparation sessions
- hackathons and team-based events

One thing I liked was seeing people who initially joined knowing almost nothing eventually start participating in contests, hackathons, open-source projects, and developer communities confidently.

That kind of ecosystem effect matters more than any single event.

---

## What I probably learned most here

Honestly, CSI shaped a lot of how I think about engineering communities.

Good technical ecosystems don't appear automatically.
Someone has to:
- organize things
- create momentum
- bring people together
- maintain standards
- keep events useful instead of performative

And once communities start compounding properly, the effect becomes very noticeable.

People collaborate more.
Projects improve.
Hackathon teams get stronger.
Knowledge spreads faster.
More people start building things seriously.

A lot of my closest technical friends, collaborators, and conversations during college came through CSI in one way or another.
